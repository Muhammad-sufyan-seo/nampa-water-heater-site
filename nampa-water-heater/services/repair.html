Gas & Electric Water Heater Repair Throughout Nampa, ID

When your water heater stops working, the cause is rarely obvious from the outside. A cold shower could mean a failed heating element, a tripped breaker, a dead thermocouple, or sediment so thick it's insulating the burner from the water. Getting the diagnosis right the first time is what separates a $200 repair from an unnecessary $1,500 replacement.

We work with licensed plumbing technicians who specialize in water heater systems—not general plumbers who handle a dozen different systems and treat water heaters as an afterthought. That focus means faster diagnostics, more accurate repair estimates, and honest guidance on whether your specific unit is worth repairing.

Common Water Heater Problems We Repair in Nampa

Canyon County's hard water supply (200–350 ppm hardness) creates repair patterns that are specific to this area. Here's what we see most often—and what it typically means for your unit:

No Hot Water

The most urgent call we receive. For gas water heaters, the most common culprits are a failed thermocouple (the safety device that keeps the pilot lit), a faulty gas valve, or a pilot light that won't stay lit due to draft or a dirty orifice. For electric water heaters, a tripped high-limit switch or a burned-out heating element is usually responsible. Both are repairable same-day in most cases.

Water Heater Leaking

Not all leaks are equal. A dripping pressure relief valve (T&P valve) is a safety device doing its job—it may need replacement or it may signal excessive pressure in the system. A leak from the tank itself, especially on a unit over 8 years old, usually means corrosion has compromised the tank lining and replacement is the right call. Leaks from connections, fittings, or the drain valve are typically straightforward repairs.

Sediment Buildup — Nampa's Hard Water Problem

If you hear rumbling, popping, or banging from your water heater, that's sediment—mineral deposits from Nampa's hard water supply that have settled on the tank floor and are now being superheated. Sediment reduces efficiency, accelerates corrosion, and can eventually crack the tank lining. Annual flushing is the best prevention; if the buildup is severe, we'll assess whether a flush will restore performance or whether the tank has already been compromised.

Pilot Light Issues

A pilot light that won't stay lit is almost always a thermocouple problem—a $20–$50 part that's a straightforward repair. Less commonly, it's a gas supply issue or a faulty gas valve. We diagnose the root cause before recommending parts.

Water Not Hot Enough

If you're getting lukewarm water instead of hot, the thermostat is the first thing to check—it may have drifted or failed. On electric units, one of two heating elements may have burned out (most electric water heaters have an upper and lower element). On gas units, a partially clogged burner or a faulty thermostat/gas valve can cause this.

Rusty or Discolored Water

Rust-colored water from the hot tap usually means the anode rod—the sacrificial magnesium or aluminum rod that protects the tank from corrosion—has been depleted. Replacing it promptly can add years to the tank's life. If the tank itself is rusting, replacement is the better path.

Tankless Water Heater Error Codes

Navien, Rinnai, and Noritz tankless units display error codes that point to specific components—ignition failures, flow sensor issues, heat exchanger scale buildup, or venting problems. We service all major tankless brands and can interpret and resolve most error codes same-day.

When to Repair vs. Replace Your Water Heater

The repair-or-replace decision comes down to three factors: the unit's age, the cost of the repair relative to a new unit, and the unit's overall condition. Our general guidance for Nampa homeowners:

  • Under 7 years old: Repair is almost always the right call unless there's tank corrosion or a catastrophic failure.
  • 7–10 years old: Evaluate repair cost vs. replacement cost. If the repair exceeds 40–50% of a new installed unit, replacement may be smarter.
  • 10+ years old: Replacement is usually the better long-term value, especially with Nampa's hard water accelerating wear. A new unit will be more efficient and come with a fresh warranty.

We'll give you this assessment honestly during the service call—not after we've already started a repair you didn't need.
